I've been working in an environment with heavy AI usage as the default for a few months now and it's interesting to notice how it affects myself and the people around me.

Besides the well-known boosts of productivity in many areas, I've been noticing **subtle changes in the economics of recognition**: If exceptional work is increasingly assumed to be AI-generated by default, the incentive to produce exceptional work may gradually weaken.

Maybe the definition of exceptional work shifts: Translation used to be an exceptional skill with exceptional outputs. Now it's easy.

The difficult think with AI is that it's much broader and harder to say what it can and cannot do. If you solve a problem where AI failed, people might still think it's AI-generated, because it's hard to see the difference.
